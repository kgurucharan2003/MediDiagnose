How to Obtain Your Health Data

Blood Pressure (Bp)

Measure your blood pressure with a home monitor or at a clinic. This value should be in millimeters of mercury (mm Hg).

Specific Gravity (Sg)

Specific gravity of urine measures the concentration of solutes in urine. It can be measured with a urine test strip or in a laboratory.

Albumin (Al)

This value represents the amount of albumin in your urine, which can be tested with a urine dipstick or in a lab.

Sugar (Su)

This value indicates the amount of sugar in your urine. It can be tested with a urine dipstick.

Red Blood Cells (Rbc)

This value represents the presence of red blood cells in your urine, which can indicate kidney issues. A urine test can measure this.

Blood Urea (Bu)

Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) levels can be determined through a blood test and indicate how well your kidneys are functioning.

Serum Creatinine (Sc)

This value measures the amount of creatinine in your blood, which helps evaluate kidney function. It is determined through a blood test.

Sodium (Sod)

Sodium levels in your blood can be tested through a blood test to assess your kidney's ability to balance salt and water.

Potassium (Pot)

This value indicates the amount of potassium in your blood. Abnormal levels can be a sign of kidney issues.

Hemoglobin (Hemo)

Hemoglobin is a protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen. Low levels can be associated with kidney disease.

White Blood Cell Count (Wbcc)

The number of white blood cells in your blood helps identify potential infections or inflammation that could be related to kidney problems.

Red Blood Cell Count (Rbcc)

This value represents the number of red blood cells in your blood, which can help assess anemia related to kidney disease.

Hypertension (Htn)

Hypertension (high blood pressure) is a common risk factor for kidney disease. Regular blood pressure checks are important.
